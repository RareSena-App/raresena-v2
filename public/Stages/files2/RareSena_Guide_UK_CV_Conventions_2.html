UK CV conventions — what to include, exclude, and never say
The UK CV is a different document from the one that got you hired at home. This is the complete translation — format, content, language, and the immigrant-specific traps that get strong candidates silently rejected
Why your excellent CV might be failing here
A CV that would win interviews in Lagos, Mumbai, Warsaw, Manila, or São Paulo can be quietly discarded in the UK — not because the experience is weaker, but because the document breaks conventions the reader does not even know they are enforcing. A photo reads as odd. A date of birth creates a discrimination-compliance problem. Ten pages of duties reads as unfocused. "Responsible for" throughout reads as passive. None of this is fair, and none of it is about your ability — it is formatting culture, and it is fully learnable in one afternoon. The rule underneath everything: a UK CV is an evidence document, not a biography. Two pages of proof that you produce results, aimed at one specific job.
The master grid — include / exclude
✓ Include
Name + contact line: email, phone, LinkedIn URL, city/region only ("Manchester, UK") — never full address
Professional summary: 3–4 sentences at the top — who you are professionally, your strongest proof, what you are aiming at
Work experience, reverse chronological: employer, role, dates (month + year), then 3–6 achievement bullets per recent role
Quantified achievements: numbers, percentages, scale — "managed a team" is a duty; "led a team of 12 through a system migration completed 3 weeks early" is evidence
Education + qualifications: degree, institution, year — with UK equivalence in brackets once you have your ENIC statement ("MSc equivalent — UK ENIC verified")
Skills section: concrete and checkable — software, languages, certifications, methodologies
UK context signals: right-to-work line if you choose to include it (see below), UK phone number, UK city
✗ Exclude — always
Photo — UK CVs never carry photos; one signals unfamiliarity and creates bias-compliance discomfort
Date of birth / age — age discrimination law makes this actively unwelcome
Marital status, children, religion, nationality — standard in many countries; never in the UK
Full home address — city is enough; full addresses are outdated and a privacy risk
"References available on request" — assumed; the line wastes your scarcest resource (space)
Salary history or expectations — negotiate later, never on the CV
Every job you have ever had — pre-2010 roles compress to one line each or vanish; relevance beats completeness
Objective statements about what YOU want — the summary is about what you offer, not what you seek
The two-page rule is real. UK recruiters spend 30–60 seconds on first screen, and two pages is the hard cultural norm (one page for early career, two for everyone else — three only in academia and medicine, where CVs follow their own conventions). If yours is longer, you are not being thorough; you are hiding your best evidence inside your weakest.

Language — the verbs that carry UK CVs
Kill "responsible for" — lead every bullet with a result verb
The single highest-impact edit. UK CV culture reads "Responsible for managing budgets" as a job description; it reads "Reduced departmental costs by 18% while maintaining service levels" as a hire signal. Strong openers: delivered, increased, reduced, built, led, launched, negotiated, redesigned, saved, secured, trained, transformed.
Before: "Responsible for customer service and handling complaints."
After: "Resolved 40+ customer escalations weekly, raising satisfaction scores from 3.2 to 4.5 in six months."
Modesty codes from home cultures ("assisted with", "was part of a team that") systematically undersell you here. If you did it, claim it plainly — plain claiming is not arrogance in UK convention; vagueness is the sin.
Translate, don't transliterate, your experience
Employer names that mean nothing here need one line of scale context: "GTBank — one of West Africa's largest banks, 8,000+ staff". Convert currencies to GBP for impact figures ("managed a ₦2bn portfolio (~£1m)"). Map titles to UK equivalents where honest ("Assistant Manager (UK equivalent: Team Leader, 15 direct reports)"). You are not diminishing your history — you are making it legible in 30 seconds.
Tailor per application — the 20-minute version
UK recruitment (human and ATS) matches your CV against the job advert's language. For each application: pull the 6–8 key phrases from the advert, make sure your genuine matching experience uses those exact words, and reorder bullets so the most relevant evidence leads. One master CV, tailored copies — never one generic CV fired at everything. This is the discipline your Task 4.3 application pipeline tracks.

Immigrant-specific traps — and the honest answers
TrapWhat to do
The right-to-work questionYou are not obliged to state immigration status on a CV. If your status needs no sponsorship (Tracks C, D, E, F — or ILR), one line helps: "Full right to work in the UK — no sponsorship required." If you DO need sponsorship, leave it off the CV and address it at application-form stage honestly — never lie, but never lead with your hardest fact either.
The overseas-experience discountCounter it with context lines (scale, rankings, revenue) and by front-loading any UK evidence you have — UK volunteering (permitted on every route), UK certifications, UK professional body membership. One line of UK evidence disproportionately reassures UK readers.
The employment gap from migrationDo not leave it silent — silence reads as concealment. One line, framed as action: "2024 — Relocated to the UK; completed ENIC qualification verification and [certification] while securing right to work." Gaps with narrative are normal; gaps without narrative are questions.
Name-based biasIt exists — studies consistently show identical CVs with minority-ethnic names receive fewer callbacks. You are NOT obliged to anglicise (see the Networking guide — keep your name). Practical mitigations that don't cost identity: LinkedIn URL prominently placed (profiles humanise), professional summary that leads with your strongest proof, and volume + referral strategies through Task 4.3 networks so no single screener controls your outcome.
Qualification confusionNever assume the reader knows your home qualification. State the UK equivalence: "(UK ENIC: equivalent to UK Bachelor's degree)". £49.50 + VAT for the ENIC statement is the highest-ROI document in your job search — Task 2.2 covers getting it.
Format mechanics — the boring rules that matter
PDF unless the advert says otherwise (Word can render differently on their machine). Filename: Firstname-Lastname-CV.pdf — never "CV_final_v3_NEW". One clean font (Calibri, Arial, or Georgia), 10.5–12pt, real margins, no tables or text boxes or graphics — ATS systems parse plain structure best and mangle designed layouts. No headers/footers carrying key information (some ATS strip them). Dates aligned consistently, month + year format. Spelling: UK English throughout ("organised", "programme", "labour") — and have one native-reader friend or your Rare Circle group proof it, because a single "recieved" undoes a page of evidence.
Never say — the five instant credibility killers: "Hardworking team player with excellent communication skills" (empty — show, never declare); any invented UK experience (verification is routine, and a caught lie is fatal plus potentially a fraud issue for sponsored workers); criticism of a previous employer; "seeking any position" (reads as desperation — aim the document); and salary figures. And one platform note: a UK CV and a LinkedIn profile are different documents — the profile can be fuller and warmer; keep them consistent but not identical, and make sure dates match exactly, because recruiters cross-check.
The 60-minute rebuild sequence
1) Strip your current CV to raw material: every role, every achievement you can evidence. 2) Write the summary last — it is the trailer, and you cannot cut a trailer before the film exists. 3) Convert every duty bullet to a result bullet with a number where truthful. 4) Apply the include/exclude grid ruthlessly. 5) Add context lines for overseas employers and ENIC equivalences. 6) Cut to two pages — if it hurts, it is working. 7) Send it to one professional in your sector for review (your Task 4.3 network, or Rare Circle). Then it enters the application pipeline — tailored per role, tracked in the app, improved by what gets responses.
