Renting in the UK as a new arrival — your rights and how to use them
The tenancy system explained: Right to Rent, deposits, tenancy types, discrimination routes, and exactly what to do when a landlord or agent is not playing fair
The system in one paragraph
UK private renting runs on a legal framework that gives you significantly more rights than most new arrivals realise — and landlords count on you not knowing them. Your deposit is legally protected money. Your landlord's access to your home is legally restricted. Discrimination against you based on nationality is illegal. And under the Renters' Rights Act reforms, "no fault" evictions are being abolished and landlords can no longer demand months of rent in advance. Knowing these rules does not just protect you — it changes how agents and landlords treat you the moment they realise you know them.
Right to Rent — what it is and what it is not
1

The landlord must check your immigration status — it is their legal duty

Before any tenancy starts in England, the landlord or agent must verify you have the right to rent. Your eVisa share code (generate at gov.uk/view-prove-immigration-status) or BRP satisfies this completely. The check is their obligation — not a favour, and not grounds for extra scrutiny of you personally.

→ Generate your share code before viewings so you can provide it on the spot. Speed signals preparedness.
2

What landlords may NOT do

They may not refuse you because of your nationality, your accent, or your name where your immigration status is valid — that is discrimination under the Equality Act 2010. They may not demand documents beyond the legal check. They may not apply different terms (higher deposit, more rent in advance) because you are foreign — the Renters' Rights Act caps advance rent at one month for everyone.

→ Keep written records of every interaction. Discrimination claims live or die on evidence.
Your core rights — the table to memorise
RightWhat it means in practice
Written tenancy agreementYou are legally entitled to written terms. No written agreement = a problem to fix, not a norm to accept. You still have rights as a periodic tenant even without one.
Deposit protection within 30 daysDeposit must go into DPS, MyDeposits, or TDS within 30 days, with "prescribed information" given to you. Breach = you can claim 1–3x the deposit in compensation.
Quiet enjoymentThe landlord cannot enter without 24 hours' written notice except genuine emergency. Unannounced visits are a breach — document and report them.
Repairs and habitabilityStructural repairs, heating, hot water, damp and mould are the landlord's responsibility. Report in writing; if ignored, escalate to your council's housing enforcement team — they can compel repairs.
Protection from retaliatory evictionEvicting you because you complained about conditions is restricted. Councils' involvement strengthens your protection.
Protection from illegal evictionChanging locks, removing belongings, cutting utilities to force you out is a criminal offence. Call the police and your council immediately if it happens.
Rent increases — process requiredRent cannot simply be raised mid-fixed-term. Increases require the proper notice process and can be challenged at tribunal if above market rate.
Viewing to move-in — the sequence that protects you
1

Before paying anything

Confirm in writing: rent, deposit amount, tenancy length, what is included. Verify the landlord or agent is real — agents should belong to a redress scheme (The Property Ombudsman or Property Redress Scheme); check their site footer. Never pay a "holding deposit" above one week's rent — that is the legal cap.

2

Before signing

Read every clause. Watch for: unfair exit fees, professional cleaning demands beyond what is lawful, and clauses letting the landlord enter freely. Ask which deposit scheme will be used — a landlord who cannot answer instantly is a red flag.

3

On move-in day

Do a full photo and video inventory — every room, every mark, every appliance, meter readings — timestamped. Email it to the landlord/agent the same day so it is on the record. This inventory is what wins deposit disputes two years later.

→ The 20 minutes of photos on day one is the highest-value action of your entire tenancy.
No credit history, no guarantor — the workarounds
Agents often demand UK credit history or a UK-based guarantor that new arrivals cannot produce. Your options, in order of preference:
1

Offer evidence instead of history

Employment contract, payslips or a job offer letter, bank statements (home country statements help), a reference from a previous landlord anywhere in the world, and proof of savings. A cover note explaining you are newly arrived with stable income converts many agents.

2

Guarantor services

Commercial guarantor services (e.g. Housing Hand, UK Guarantor) act as your guarantor for a fee — typically 50–75% of one month's rent per year. Cheaper than the common alternative demand of six months upfront, and now that advance rent is capped, a useful lever.

3

If an agent demands months of rent upfront

Under the Renters' Rights Act, landlords cannot require more than one month's rent in advance. Quote it calmly:

"My understanding is that advance rent is capped at one month under the Renters' Rights Act. I can provide employment evidence and a guarantor service instead. Shall we proceed on that basis?"

The single most important rule: never leave a property just because a landlord tells you to. Only a court order can end your right to stay. Landlords rely on new arrivals not knowing this. Call Shelter (0808 800 4444, free) before making any decision about leaving.
Track E — additional notes for refugees
If you have just received status and are in the 28-day move-on window: contact your council's housing options team on day one (homelessness prevention duty starts before you are homeless), call Migrant Help (0808 8010 503), and apply for Universal Credit immediately — the housing element helps cover rent. The Refugee Council's move-on support can advocate with landlords who are hesitant about tenants transitioning from asylum support.
