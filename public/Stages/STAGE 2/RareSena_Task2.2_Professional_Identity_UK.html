🔭 Stage 2 — Rediscover
Task 2.2 — Map your professional identity to the UK market
⏱ 45–60 min  ·  💼 Your skills are real. Now translate them.  ·  All tracks
Why this matters
Your qualifications and experience are real. The work you did, the problems you solved, the expertise you built — none of that disappeared when you crossed a border. But the UK market does not automatically recognise it. It has its own frameworks, its own conventions, and its own gatekeeping systems that were not designed with you in mind.

Underemployment is the defining professional struggle for immigrants in the UK. Research consistently shows that around a third of migrants are over-qualified for the jobs they hold — and that this is not about ability. It is about translation. The person who was a senior engineer, a practising lawyer, a hospital consultant, or a published academic in their home country can end up stacking shelves or working in a call centre — not because they lack the skills, but because nobody told them how the UK system works and what steps are needed to re-enter it.

This task tells you exactly what you are working with and what translation steps apply to your situation.
What to do
  1. Use the skills inventory below to list your top 3 professional skills and your highest qualification. Be specific — not "management experience" but "managed a team of 12 engineers across two countries for three years."
  2. Check whether your qualification needs a UK ENIC Statement of Comparability. This is the official document that tells UK employers, universities, and professional bodies how your overseas qualification compares to UK standards. Cost: approximately £50–£100 + VAT. Processing: 15 working days standard, faster for express. Apply at enic.org.uk.
  3. Check whether your profession requires UK registration before you can practise. Medicine, nursing, dentistry, law, teaching, engineering, social work, pharmacy, and architecture all have separate regulatory bodies with their own requirements. See the table below.
  4. Use the professional summary builder below to draft your UK professional summary — a paragraph that describes who you are, what you do, and what you are looking for, in UK language and conventions.

Regulated professions — UK registration requirements
If your profession appears below, you cannot legally practise in the UK without completing the UK registration process — regardless of your overseas qualifications or experience. The Statement of Comparability is a separate step from professional registration. You need both.
ProfessionUK Regulatory BodyKey requirement
Medicine (doctors)General Medical Council (GMC) — gmc-uk.orgPLAB test (or recognised primary medical qualification) + GMC registration. International Medical Graduates (IMGs) must complete PLAB 1 and PLAB 2.
Nursing and midwiferyNursing and Midwifery Council (NMC) — nmc.org.ukCBT (Computer Based Test) + OSCE (Objective Structured Clinical Examination) for overseas nurses. UK Nursing Association registration required before any clinical work.
DentistryGeneral Dental Council (GDC) — gdc-uk.orgORE (Overseas Registration Examination) for non-EEA qualified dentists. Two-part examination. Significant waiting times.
PharmacyGeneral Pharmaceutical Council (GPhC) — pharmacyregulation.orgOverseas Pharmacists Assessment Programme (OSPAP) for non-EEA pharmacists. One-year postgraduate diploma at a UK university required.
Law (solicitors)Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA) — sra.org.ukQualified Lawyers Transfer Scheme (QLTS) — now the Solicitors Qualifying Examination (SQE). Overseas qualified lawyers must pass SQE1 and SQE2 and complete 2 years qualifying work experience.
TeachingTeaching Regulation Agency (TRA) — gov.uk/government/organisations/teaching-regulation-agencyQualified Teacher Status (QTS) required to teach in maintained schools. Overseas trained teachers can apply for QTS assessment. Scotland, Wales and NI have separate processes.
EngineeringEngineering Council — engineeringcouncil.orgChartered Engineer (CEng) or Incorporated Engineer (IEng) status via a licensed professional engineering institution. UK ENIC Statement of Comparability typically required as part of application.
Social workSocial Work England — socialworkengland.org.ukRegistration required. Overseas social workers must have qualifications assessed and demonstrate English language proficiency. Scotland, Wales and NI have separate regulators.
ArchitectureArchitects Registration Board (ARB) — arb.org.ukOverseas architects must have qualifications recognised by ARB. Part 1, Part 2 and Part 3 equivalence required. Process can take 12–24 months.
Accountancy (ICAEW, ACCA, CIMA)Individual professional bodiesNot formally regulated by law but professional membership is expected by most employers. Overseas accountants can apply for credit exemptions based on existing qualifications.
Other professionsVariousCheck the government's list of regulated professions at gov.uk/guidance/regulated-professions-in-the-uk before assuming you can practise freely.
UK CV conventions — what to include and what to leave out
✓ Include
Name, email, phone, LinkedIn URL, city/region (not full address)
Professional summary — 3–4 sentences, first person, present tense
Work experience in reverse chronological order with bullet-pointed achievements (not duties)
Quantified results wherever possible ("reduced processing time by 40%")
Education with institution, degree, year
Professional memberships and certifications
UK ENIC Statement of Comparability reference if overseas degree
Right to work in the UK statement (1 line: "Full right to work in the UK — [visa type]")
✗ Leave out
Photograph — illegal to require in the UK, and including one can trigger unconscious bias
Date of birth — discrimination law protects against age bias
Marital status or family information
Nationality or country of origin (separately from right to work)
Full home address
An "Objective" section — UK CVs do not use these
References — write "References available on request" only
Academic transcripts (attach separately only if specifically requested)

What to expect to feel
Mapping your professional identity to a new market can bring up something close to grief. You built expertise over years. You were good at what you did. And now you are being asked to translate, re-qualify, and prove yourself again to a system that does not yet know you. That is not failure. That is the cost of arrival — and it is a cost that says nothing about your capability and everything about the system's lack of portability. The work you do in this task starts building the bridge.
