How to check if your deposit is protected
A 10-minute check that could be worth up to three times your deposit — and how to claim if your landlord broke the rules
The law in one paragraph
If you rent under an assured shorthold tenancy in England or Wales and paid a deposit, your landlord was legally required to do two things within 30 days of receiving your money: protect it in one of three government-approved schemes, and give you the "prescribed information" — a document naming the scheme, the amount, and how to get it back. If they failed either duty, a court can order them to pay you between one and three times the deposit — and it can also block them from using a Section 21 eviction against you. This applies even if you have already moved out (claims can go back up to 6 years).
The three schemes — check all of them
DPS
depositprotection.com
Deposit Protection Service. Free "Is my deposit protected?" search — surname, postcode, tenancy start date, deposit amount.
MyDeposits
mydeposits.co.uk
Deposit checker on homepage. Same details required.
TDS
tenancydepositscheme.com
Tenancy Deposit Scheme. "Is my deposit protected?" tool. Also covers many letting-agent-held deposits.
Why all three: the landlord chooses the scheme and rarely tells you which. A "not found" in one scheme means nothing until you have checked all three — and checked with the exact tenancy details (deposits are sometimes registered under one tenant's name only, or under the agent's name).
The check — step by step
1

Gather four details

Your surname (and other tenants' surnames), the property postcode, the tenancy start date, and the deposit amount. All are on your tenancy agreement.

2

Run the search on all three scheme websites

Takes about three minutes each. Try variations: your surname vs a joint tenant's surname; agent name if you rented through one.

3

Found? Verify the details and save the certificate

Check the amount matches what you paid and the tenancy dates are right. Download or screenshot the confirmation. Also confirm you received the prescribed information — if you never got it, the landlord is still in breach even though the money is protected.

→ Add the certificate to your document folder — it is also address evidence for your ILR file.
4

Not found in any scheme? Write to the landlord first

Give them the chance to fix or explain — some deposits are protected late or under odd references. Send this:

"I paid a deposit of £[amount] on [date] for [address]. I have checked all three government-approved schemes and cannot find it registered, and I have not received the prescribed information. Please confirm within 14 days which scheme holds my deposit and provide the prescribed information. I am aware of my rights under the Housing Act 2004."
5

No satisfactory answer? You have a claim

Contact Shelter (0808 800 4444) or Citizens Advice for free guidance on a county court claim for 1–3x the deposit. Many landlords settle the moment a letter before action arrives, because the law is clearly against them. You do not need to still live in the property, and using a no-win-no-fee tenancy deposit solicitor is common.

Getting your deposit back at the end of a tenancy
Before you leave
Clean to the standard of your move-in inventory, photograph every room and meter reading on the way out, return all keys and get written confirmation.
Day of leaving
Request the deposit back in writing. The landlord then has 10 days to return it or state proposed deductions.
If deductions proposed
Ask for itemised evidence (invoices, quotes). "Professional cleaning" without receipts, and wear-and-tear charges, are routinely rejected by adjudicators.
If you disagree
Use the scheme's free dispute resolution. The landlord must PROVE the deduction — your move-in photos from day one are your winning evidence. Adjudication is free, written, and binding.
Common trap: do not accept a partial refund "to close things quickly" before seeing evidence for deductions. Fair wear and tear is not deductible — a tenancy leaves normal traces, and the law expects landlords to absorb them.
