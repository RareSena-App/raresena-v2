The ILR evidence file — what to keep and how to organise it
ILR applications fail on evidence more than eligibility. This is the file that makes yours unfailable — built over years, not panic-assembled at month 58
The principle
When you apply for Indefinite Leave to Remain, the Home Office is testing a claim: that you have lawfully and continuously resided in the UK for your qualifying period, met your route's conditions throughout, and meet the application requirements. You will have lived that truth for five years — but living it is not the same as proving it. The applicants who suffer are rarely rule-breakers; they are people who cannot produce a tenancy agreement from 2023, whose old employer's payroll portal deleted their payslips, whose travel dates live only in memory. The fix costs nothing: a folder, a habit, and this guide. Companion tool: the app's ILR Evidence File Checklist tracks your completeness percentage; this guide explains the what, why, and how.
The folder structure — set it up today
📁 ILR-Evidence/
├── 01-Identity-Immigration/ — passports (all), eVisa screenshots, every Home Office letter
├── 02-Travel-Absences/ — absence log export, boarding passes/stamps for long trips
├── 03-Employment-Income/
│ ├── 2024/ — payslips (12), P60, contract
│ ├── 2025/ — one sub-folder per year, filled monthly
│ └── 2026/
├── 04-Address-Residence/
│ ├── 2024/ — tenancy, council tax, 2–3 utility bills, quarterly bank statements
│ └── …per year
├── 05-Tax-HMRC/ — SA returns/SA302s if self-employed, tax account screenshots
├── 06-Application-Requirements/ — Life in the UK pass, English evidence, fee savings plan
├── 07-Family/ — marriage cert, children's documents, cohabitation evidence (Track C)
└── 08-Route-Specific/ — sponsor letters (A/H), endorsement records (F/G), status grant (E)
Two copies, always: one cloud folder (Google Drive/OneDrive — named exactly as above) and one physical folder for originals and anything with a wet signature. Feed both monthly. A document you cannot find in five minutes does not exist.
What each route needs most — the emphasis table
TrackEvidence that decides YOUR application
A — Skilled WorkerSalary continuity: every payslip, every P60, contracts and change letters proving you always met threshold and role. Sponsor change letters if you moved employers.
C — Family/SpouseRelationship subsistence across the whole period: joint tenancy/bills/accounts, correspondence to the same address spread across every year — not clustered in application month.
D — BN(O)Continuous residence: address evidence per year plus meticulous absence records (home-country visits add up faster than people think).
E — RefugeeStatus grant letter (the residence clock and route terms flow from it), plus standard residence evidence. Travel documents history if you travelled.
F — Global TalentEvidence of earnings/activity in your endorsed field across the period — contracts, invoices, publications, roles. The 3-year route especially compresses the timeline: start on day one.
G — Innovator FounderBusiness records proving ILR milestones (remember: any TWO of the four options — the £50,000 investment figure is one option, not a requirement): accounts, investment records, job-creation payroll, revenue records, contact-point meeting confirmations.
H — Self-SponsoredBoth sides: personal (payslips at threshold, role evidence) AND company (sponsor licence records, SMS reports, company accounts, board minutes). Home Office scrutiny on this route is elevated — over-document.
The five classic evidence failures — and their fixes
Failure 1: The vanished payslips
You left an employer; their portal access closed; three years of payslips gone. Reconstructing them later means begging an old HR department that may not respond.
✓ Fix: download every payslip as PDF in the month it is issued. Two minutes, monthly, forever.
Failure 2: The unlogged travel
The application asks for your complete travel history with dates. Memory fails; passport stamps are incomplete in the e-gates era; one wrong date contradicts Home Office records and undermines credibility.
✓ Fix: the app's Visa & ILR Countdown Tracker absence log — enter every trip within a week of returning. Export it yearly to folder 02.
Failure 3: The year with no address evidence
A year of living with family or an all-bills-included room leaves no council tax, no utilities, no tenancy in your name — a residence gap on paper.
✓ Fix: build alternatives in real time — bank statements to that address, GP letters, official correspondence, a landlord/host letter. Redirect all official mail to wherever you actually live, always.
Failure 4: The HMRC mismatch
Income declared to the Home Office differs from HMRC records — often from an innocent error years earlier. This triggers refusals on credibility grounds.
✓ Fix: check your personal tax account (gov.uk) annually against your own records; correct discrepancies the year they happen, not at application.
Failure 5: The last-minute requirements
Life in the UK test centres booked out; English test expired; £3,029 fee (check current rate) not saved — eligibility reached but application delayed months, sometimes into a visa expiry crunch.
✓ Fix: sit the Life in the UK test any time from year 3 (it never expires); check your English evidence type early (a degree-taught-in-English confirmation via ENIC never expires either); make the fee a named Task 3.5 savings goal 18 months out.
The maintenance ritual
1

Monthly (2 minutes)

Payslip PDF into the year folder. Any Home Office/official letter scanned in. New trips logged.

2

Quarterly (10 minutes — attach to a Task 3.3 weekly review)

One bank statement into the address folder. Checklist tool updated. Anything new (tenancy renewal, contract change) filed.

3

Annually (20 minutes)

P60 in. HMRC account cross-checked. Absence log exported. New year sub-folders created. Completeness percentage reviewed against your ILR countdown.

And 2–3 months before applying: book a single review session with an OISC-regulated adviser to check the assembled file against your route's current requirements — rules change, and a one-off review costs a fraction of a refused application (the fee is not refunded on refusal).
