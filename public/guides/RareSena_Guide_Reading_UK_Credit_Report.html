Reading your UK credit report — Experian, Equifax, TransUnion
What each section means, why your three scores disagree, the errors that quietly sabotage immigrants' files, and how to fix them

Why your three scores disagree — and why that is normal: the agencies are competing companies with separate databases. Lenders report to one, two, or all three — so each agency sees a different slice of your life, updates on different days, and scores on a different scale. A 720 at TransUnion is excellent; at Experian it is poor. Never compare numbers across agencies — compare each number against its own band, and track all three (the app's Credit Score Tracker does this).
Anatomy of your report — section by section
👤
1. Personal information
Name, date of birth, current and previous addresses, and aliases. Lenders match applications against this — inconsistency here causes rejections that have nothing to do with your creditworthiness.
⚠ Immigrant-specific traps: name spelled differently across accounts (transliteration variations — "Mohammed/Muhammad", missing middle names); addresses in the wrong format from when you first arrived; a previous address showing a flat number wrongly. Every variation fragments your file. Standardise ONE spelling of your name and ONE address format, and get every account to match it.
🗳️
2. Electoral roll
Confirms whether you are registered to vote at your current address. This is the single heaviest identity-confidence signal in UK credit scoring.
✓ Eligibility surprise: Irish and qualifying Commonwealth citizens resident in the UK CAN register to vote (and thus join the electoral roll) — this includes Nigerians, Ghanaians, Indians, Pakistanis, Jamaicans, and citizens of 50+ Commonwealth states. EU citizens can register for local elections. Register at gov.uk/register-to-vote — the score effect appears within about 4–6 weeks. If you are not eligible, some agencies accept a notice of correction confirming your residence; and lenders can still verify you by other means — but this is why building the rest of the file matters more for you.
💳
3. Credit accounts (the core of the file)
Every credit card, loan, mortgage, overdraft, phone contract, and some utilities — each showing the lender, open date, credit limit, balance, and a month-by-month payment history grid (0 = paid on time; 1–6 = months in arrears; D = default; the grid is what lenders actually read).
✓ What good looks like: a row of 0s. Utilisation under 30% of each limit. Accounts ageing — the older your oldest account, the better, which is why you never close your first UK credit card.
⚠ Check for: accounts you do not recognise (possible fraud or a stranger's data merged into your file — common with shared names), wrong balances, a missed-payment marker you dispute, and a phone contract from your first month that you forgot about.
🔍
4. Searches (footprints)
Hard searches (credit applications) are visible to lenders and suppress your score slightly for ~3–6 months; several in a short window signals distress. Soft searches (eligibility checkers, your own checks, employer checks) are visible only to you and never affect the score.
✓ Rule: always use eligibility checkers (soft) before applying (hard). Never make two credit applications in the same month while your file is thin.
🔗
5. Financial associations
People you share joint accounts, joint applications, or a joint mortgage with. Their credit behaviour influences lenders' view of YOU.
⚠ If you are financially linked to someone with poor credit (an ex, an old flatmate from a joint bill), and the account is closed, apply to each agency for a "notice of disassociation" — it removes the link.
⚖️
6. Public records
CCJs (County Court Judgments), bankruptcies, IVAs. A CCJ stays for six years — but if you pay it within one month of the judgment, it is removed entirely.
⚠ Immigrant trap: CCJs are sometimes issued to an old address you left — for a final utility bill or parking charge you never saw — and you discover them only when a mortgage is refused years later. This is why you check all three reports quarterly, and why you always redirect mail and give final meter readings when moving.
Fixing errors — the dispute process
1

Dispute with the agency, in writing, with evidence

All three have online dispute processes. State what is wrong and attach proof (statements, correspondence). They must investigate — usually responding within 28 days — and mark the entry as disputed meanwhile.

2

Go to the lender in parallel

The data belongs to the lender that reported it; they can correct it at source, which fixes all three agencies at once.

3

Escalate if unresolved

Add a "notice of correction" (a 200-word statement on your file telling your side), and complain to the Financial Ombudsman Service (free) if the lender will not correct a genuine error.

Quarterly ritual (add it to a Task 3.3 weekly review each quarter): open all three reports, scan the six sections above, dispute anything wrong immediately. Fifteen minutes, four times a year — it is the difference between discovering an error today and discovering it inside a mortgage application.
